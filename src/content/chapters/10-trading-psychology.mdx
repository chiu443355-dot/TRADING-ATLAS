<p class="ch-intro">Your biggest enemy in trading isn't the market. It's your brain. Emotions like fear, greed, and FOMO cause more losses than any bad strategy.</p>
<div class="g2">
  <div class="card glass-red"><h3>Revenge Trading</h3><p style="margin-bottom:10px">After a loss, you feel the urge to "get it back" immediately. You take a bigger trade, driven by emotion. One loss becomes three.</p><p><strong style="color:#fff">Fix:</strong> After ANY loss, mandatory 1-hour break. Walk away from the screen.</p></div>
  <div class="card glass-teal"><h3>FOMO Trading</h3><p style="margin-bottom:10px">You see a massive candle already 100 pips into a move. You chase it, entering late. Price then reverses on you.</p><p><strong style="color:#fff">Fix:</strong> If you missed the entry, accept it. The market creates new opportunities every day.</p></div>
  <div class="card glass-purple"><h3>Overtrading</h3><p style="margin-bottom:10px">Staring at charts for hours with no valid setup. Boredom creeps in. You take a trade just to trade. This is gambling disguised as trading.</p><p><strong style="color:#fff">Fix:</strong> Define your checklist before sitting down. No setup = no trade. Full stop.</p></div>
  <div class="card glass-gold"><h3>Early Profit Taking</h3><p style="margin-bottom:10px">Your trade is up 10 pips. You close early, making $5 instead of your planned $30 target.</p><p><strong style="color:#fff">Fix:</strong> Place your take profit BEFORE entering. Don't watch the trade live.</p></div>
</div>
<div class="card glass-gold" style="margin-top:8px">
  <h3>The Dopamine Trap — Explained Neurologically</h3>
  <p style="margin-bottom:14px">Every trade triggers dopamine — the same chemical that makes gambling addictive. WIN → dopamine → overconfidence → bigger risk → loss. LOSS → cortisol → anxiety → revenge trade → bigger loss. This neurochemical cycle destroys most traders before they can develop an edge.</p>
  <p style="margin-bottom:14px"><strong style="color:#fff">The neuroscience:</strong> Your amygdala (fear center) fires identically for a losing trade as it does for a physical threat. Your prefrontal cortex (rational thought) is suppressed during high emotional states — meaning you literally cannot think clearly while watching a losing trade.</p>
  <p><strong style="color:#fff">The mind reset:</strong> After every trade (win OR loss), step away 10–15 minutes. Exercise, cold water, watch something funny. This breaks the dopamine/cortisol cycle and resets your emotional state to neutral before the next decision.</p>
</div>
<div style="margin-top:28px">
  <div style="font-family:'Fira Code',monospace;font-size:10px;color:var(--gold);letter-spacing:.3em;margin-bottom:16px;">6 PSYCHOLOGICAL LAWS OF TRADING</div>
  <div class="psych-law"><h4>Law 1: Certainty is your enemy</h4><p>The moment you are "sure" a trade will work, you are most vulnerable. The market owes you nothing. All trades are probabilities, not certainties.</p></div>
  <div class="psych-law"><h4>Law 2: Losers are data, not failures</h4><p>A losing trade that followed your system is not a failure. It is one data point in a sample of hundreds. Judge your system over 50+ trades, never over 5.</p></div>
  <div class="psych-law"><h4>Law 3: Winners are dangerous</h4><p>A winning streak creates a false sense of invincibility. More traders blow accounts after a 5-win run than after a 5-loss run. Wins breed overconfidence.</p></div>
  <div class="psych-law"><h4>Law 4: Boredom is more costly than fear</h4><p>Fear prevents you from entering good trades. But boredom drives you to enter bad ones. Most retail traders overtrade from boredom, not from greed.</p></div>
  <div class="psych-law"><h4>Law 5: Your identity must not be your P&L</h4><p>Traders who tie their self-worth to daily profit/loss cannot make objective decisions. Your identity as a person has nothing to do with whether you made 10 pips today.</p></div>
  <div class="psych-law"><h4>Law 6: Process over outcome</h4><p>You can follow your system perfectly and lose. You can break every rule and win. What matters is whether you executed your process correctly — the outcome is irrelevant to evaluation.</p></div>
</div>
